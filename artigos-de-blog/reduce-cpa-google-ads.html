How to reduce CPA by 30% without cutting lead volume

Smart segmentation, tested creatives and an optimised account structure to lower acquisition cost without sacrificing the flow of qualified contacts.

S
Sica Team Sica Creative
7 April 2026 · 6 min read

Reducing CPA is not about spending less — it's about spending better. Most Google Ads accounts have systematic losses that go unnoticed. In this article we show where they are, how to identify them and how to fix them without sacrificing volume.

1. Diagnosis: where is the waste

Before optimising, you need to understand what is pushing CPA up. In most accounts we find three main sources of inefficiency.

The first is overly broad targeting. Broad match campaigns without well-crafted negative keywords capture irrelevant traffic that consumes budget without converting. The second is a confusing ad group structure, which prevents the algorithm from learning from clean data. The third is the absence of creative testing, which keeps the account at a mediocre performance level.

💡
Practical tip

Before touching bids or budgets, export the search terms report for the last 30 days. Sort by cost descending and identify the 20% of terms consuming the most without converting.

2. Account structure that enables optimisation

A well-structured account is the foundation of everything. Without it, the algorithm cannot learn and the manager cannot isolate variables to make decisions.

The fundamental principle is one goal per campaign. Mixing micro-conversions with macro-conversions in the same campaign confuses the bidding system and dilutes learning data.

  • Separate campaigns by intent (brand vs. non-brand vs. competitor)
  • Group ads by tight semantic theme, not by volume
  • Maintain at least 30–50 conversions per campaign per month for automation to work
  • Use audience segments as observation before applying differentiated bids

Account structure is the skeleton of the system. Without it, any optimisation is temporary and gains disappear when the manager changes.

3. Segmentation by real intent

The biggest waste in Google Ads is appearing for searches with the wrong intent. A user searching "what is CAC" is in a completely different mindset from someone searching "digital marketing agency Lisbon pricing".

Intent-based separation allows you to allocate budget where the probability of conversion is highest, and treat the remaining segments with different messages and bids.

–28% Average CPA reduction after ad group restructuring
+41% Increase in leads after adding intent-based negative keyword list
3.1x ROAS improvement in accounts with brand vs. non-brand campaign split

4. Creatives: the most undervalued factor

Bids and structure optimise where and when you appear. Creatives determine whether the user clicks. A more relevant ad has a higher CTR, which improves Quality Score, which lowers CPC, which lowers CPA. It's a self-feeding cycle.

The methodology we use is simple: test one variable at a time with enough volume to reach statistical significance.

  1. Headline 1 — test the main value proposition
  2. Headline 2 — test social proof (number, client type, result)
  3. Headline 3 — test the call to action (urgency vs. benefit)
  4. Description 1 — test the problem-led vs. solution-led approach

5. Bidding: automate without losing control

Google's automated bidding strategies are now very capable — but they only work well with clean and sufficient data. Activating Target CPA on an account with 5 conversions per month is counterproductive.

The correct approach is a progression: Maximise Clicks → Manual CPA → Target CPA → Target ROAS. Each transition should only happen when there is statistically relevant data.

⚠️
Common mistake

Changing the bidding strategy while simultaneously adjusting creatives and negatives. When something changes, change only one variable — that way you know what caused the effect.

Conclusion: a process, not a one-off action

Reducing CPA sustainably is not the result of a one-time adjustment. It is the result of a systematic process of diagnosis, testing and continuous optimisation. The accounts that grow most are those with the highest iteration rate — not necessarily the largest budget.
